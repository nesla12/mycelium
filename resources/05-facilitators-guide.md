# The Facilitator's Guide
## How to run hard debates with children without it becoming chaos, or your opinion

### Companion to Pillar 3, Part II. Free to use, adapt, translate, and fork.
#### Grounded in Philosophy for Children, the community of inquiry, and dialogic teaching.

---

# Why this guide exists

The advanced module asks children to debate questions that have no approved answer. That is powerful in skilled hands and a mess in unskilled ones. The difference is not the questions. It is the facilitation. This guide gives you the stance, the moves, and the troubleshooting to run these sessions well, even if you have never done it before.

The tradition behind it is old and tested. Fifty years ago Matthew Lipman turned the classroom into a community of inquiry, where the teacher guides the search rather than supplying the answer. Decades of practice since have worked out how to do it, including how to handle the exact problems you will hit: the silence, the chaos, the child who dominates, the child who provokes. You are not starting from scratch. You are joining a craft.

The goal of a session is not a winner. It is a community thinking together, where children reason, listen, build on each other, and sometimes change their minds. A child changing their mind because of a good argument is the single best thing that can happen in the room. Name it as a victory, not a defeat.

---

# The one shift that makes or breaks it

You have spent your career being the person with the answer. For these sessions you have to stop. Your authority does not go away, it changes job: from giving answers to protecting the quality of thinking.

This is genuinely hard, and the hardest part is keeping your own opinion out of it. The moment children sense which answer you favor, most of them will chase it, and the inquiry dies. So on the contested questions, you withhold your view, on purpose, all the way through.

Two reasons this matters, worth telling yourself before every session. First, a child who reasoned their own way to a view holds it more deeply and is far harder to manipulate than one who guessed yours. Second, the contested questions in this module genuinely do not have a settled answer, so handing them one would be a lie dressed as teaching.

# Set the conditions before you start

**Co-create the ground rules with the class.** Rules they wrote, they keep. Aim for four or five, in their words. Typical ones worth steering toward:
- One person speaks at a time, and we listen to understand, not just to reply.
- We attack ideas, never people.
- It is good to change your mind. That is what thinking is for.
- Everyone's reasons matter, and you can always ask someone why.
- It is fine to be unsure, and fine to pass.

**Sit in a circle.** Everyone should see everyone. The circle quietly says this is a conversation among equals, not a performance aimed at you. Step slightly out of the circle yourself when the dialogue is flowing, to signal that it belongs to them.

**Build safety first.** Children will only risk a real opinion if it is safe to be wrong. Praise the courage of a half-formed thought. Protect anyone who gets laughed at. Make "I changed my mind" a thing that earns respect in your room.

# The facilitator's toolbox: moves you can say out loud

This is the core craft. These are ready-to-use sentences. Print this section. Keep it in your hand. You do not improvise brilliant questions, you reach for the right move.

**To launch.**
- "What do you notice? What questions does this raise for you?"
- "Take thirty seconds of silence to think before anyone speaks."

**To press for reasoning** (the most important move, use constantly).
- "What makes you say that?"
- "What is your reason?"
- "How do you know?"
- "Can you give an example?"

**To build the dialogue between children, not through you.**
- "Who can build on what they just said?"
- "Does anyone want to respond to that?"
- "Maria, what do you think of what Tom just argued?"
- (Physically open your hand toward another child rather than answering yourself.)

**To challenge and deepen** (raise the difficulty).
- "What is the strongest argument against your own view?"
- "Can someone give the best case for the other side?"
- "What would have to be true for that to be wrong?"
- "Push that further. So what follows?"

**To surface hidden assumptions.**
- "What are we assuming here?"
- "Is that always true, or only sometimes?"
- "What do you mean exactly by that word?"

**To manage airtime.**
- To a dominator: "Thank you, that is a lot to think about. Let's hear from someone who hasn't spoken."
- To the quiet: "We haven't heard from this side of the circle. Any thoughts, or a question?"
- "Turn to the person next to you and say your thought to them first." (Lowers the stakes, then come back.)

**To slow down and reflect** (this also buys you time).
- "Let me make sure I understood. You are saying ...?" (Revoice their point, then check.)
- "Where are we now? What have we figured out, and what is still open?"

**To test the room.**
- "Hands up who agrees. Now who sees it differently? Why?"
- "Has anyone changed their mind in the last ten minutes? What changed it?"

**To close.**
- "What is one thing you are taking away, even if you are still unsure?"
- "What question are we leaving unanswered for next time?"

# Staying neutral: keeping your opinion out

**When a child asks "But what do YOU think, teacher?"** This will happen. Do not answer, and do not be evasive about why. Try:
- "I will tell you at the very end, maybe. Right now my job is to help you think, not to think for you."
- "It matters more what you think and why. Convince me. What's your reason?"
- "I can argue any side of this. Which side do you want me to challenge?"

**The devil's advocate move.** When one side is winning too easily, take up the losing side yourself, openly: "Let me play the other side for a moment, just to test it." This keeps the inquiry honest without revealing what you actually believe. Children quickly learn you will challenge whoever is comfortable.

**Validate reasoning, not conclusions.** Never say "good answer." Say "good reason" or "interesting, can you back that up?" You are praising the thinking, which keeps you neutral on the content.

**Watch your face and your "mm-hmm."** Children read tiny signals. A raised eyebrow at one view and a warm nod at another tells them your answer without a word. Keep your reactions even.

# Three session formats you can run

**1. The inquiry circle (the default).** Show a stimulus or pose the question. Silent thinking time. First responses, one at a time. Then open dialogue, you using the moves above to press, build, and challenge. End with a reflection round. Best for depth.

**2. The opinion line.** Mark one wall "strongly agree" and the opposite "strongly disagree." Read a claim. Children physically stand on the line where they fall. Ask a few to say why they stand there. Then invite anyone to move if an argument shifts them, visibly. Best for energy, movement, and making changing-your-mind literal.

**3. The fishbowl.** A small inner group debates while the outer group observes silently, then they swap, or the outer group critiques the quality of the reasoning they saw. Best for teaching what good dialogue looks like, and for large or rowdy classes.

# Troubleshooting: the hard moments

This is the part most guides skip. Keep it nearby.

**The silence. Nobody talks.** Do not fill it, and do not panic. Silence is thinking. Wait longer than feels comfortable. Then lower the stakes: "Turn to your neighbor and say one thought." Or offer a forced choice: "A or B? Just point." Starting with a stance is easier than starting with an essay.

**The chaos. Everyone talks at once.** Stop and restate one ground rule, calmly: "One voice. We listen to understand." Use a talking object that must be held to speak. If it is genuinely overheated, switch to writing for two minutes, then resume.

**One or two children dominate.** Thank them sincerely, then redirect by name to others. Use turn-and-talk so the quiet ones rehearse a thought before the whole group. If needed, a gentle private word after: "Your ideas are great. I need you to help me pull the others in."

**The provocateur, saying something to shock.** Do not react with alarm, which is the reward they want. Treat it as a claim to be reasoned about: "Okay, that is a strong claim. What is your reason?" Often the provocation collapses when asked for a reason. Often it becomes a real contribution.

**A factual error stated confidently.** First, let the group catch it: "Does everyone agree that's accurate? How could we check?" Self-correction by peers teaches more than your correction. If no one catches it and it matters, supply the fact plainly, then return to the reasoning.

**Everyone agrees too fast (groupthink).** This is a failure, not a success. Break it: "We all agree, which is suspicious. Who can argue the opposite, even if you don't believe it?" Or take the other side yourself. Comfortable consensus is where thinking stops.

**It gets personal, or a child is upset.** Pause the content immediately. Restate the rule: ideas, not people. Check on the child. If a friendship or feelings are involved, that takes priority over finishing the debate. You can always say: "Let's leave this question here for today."

# The limits of neutrality: where you stop facilitating and start protecting

Read this section before you run a single debate. Your neutrality applies to contested ideas. It never applies to a child's safety or dignity. The two stances are different jobs, and confusing them is dangerous.

You step out of the neutral facilitator role at once, every time, when:

- **A child says something that dehumanizes or targets a person or group.** You do not "both-sides" the dignity of a human being. Name it clearly and calmly: "We question ideas here, and we do not attack people or groups. That is a line." Protect anyone targeted in the room. Then, if useful, you can turn it into reasoning: "Where does a belief like that come from, and who does it harm?" But the protection comes first, not the inquiry.

- **A child discloses harm, danger, or something that worries you about their safety or another child's.** The debate is over for you in that moment. Follow your school's safeguarding procedure. This overrides everything in this guide.

- **A child is in real distress.** Care comes before curriculum. Stop, support, and if needed end the session.

- **Anyone encourages real harm to themselves or others.** You do not stay neutral. You intervene.

Say this to your class once, plainly, at the start of the term: "In here you can question almost anything, and I will not tell you what to think. But we never attack who people are, and if something serious comes up, I will step in, because protecting you matters more than any debate." Children trust the freedom more when they can see the floor under it.

# How to assess a debate

You are not grading who won. You are noticing the quality of thinking. Watch for, and over time look for growth in:
- Giving reasons without being asked.
- Building on or genuinely responding to others, rather than just waiting to talk.
- Considering a view they disagree with, fairly.
- Changing a position because of an argument, and being able to say why.
- Asking good questions, not just giving answers.

A simple record: after a session, jot which children reasoned, built, challenged well, or shifted. Over a term you will see the room get sharper. That is the result.

---

# Getting good at this yourself

Facilitation is a skill, learned by doing, and you will be clumsy at first. That is fine. Three things speed it up. Start with lower-stakes questions before the hot ones. Resist the old reflex of question-answer-praise, which shuts thinking down, in favor of question-and-wait. And occasionally record yourself, or ask a colleague to watch, and count how often you talked versus the children. The aim, over time, is less of you and more of them.

# The one-page cheat sheet

**Remember five things.**
1. Your job is to protect the thinking, not to supply the answer.
2. Keep your own opinion out. Withhold, deflect, play devil's advocate.
3. Praise reasons, never conclusions.
4. Less you, more them. Wait through silence. Pass questions to other children.
5. Neutral on ideas, never neutral on safety or dignity.

**The eight moves you will use most.**
- "What makes you say that?"
- "Can you give an example?"
- "Who can build on that?"
- "What's the strongest argument against your own view?"
- "What are we assuming here?"
- "So you're saying ...?" (revoice and check)
- "Who sees it differently?"
- "Has anyone changed their mind? Why?"

# Grounding and sources

- Philosophy for Children and the community of inquiry (Matthew Lipman; Splitter and Sharp; SAPERE handbooks): the facilitative, non-authoritative role and the discipline of collective inquiry.
- The Socratic tradition of teaching by question rather than assertion.
- Dialogic teaching and accountable-talk research on discussion moves such as pressing for reasoning and revoicing, and on breaking the question-answer-praise pattern that suppresses thinking.
- Evidence linking well-facilitated philosophical dialogue to gains in reasoning and to improved peer interaction, alongside the documented need for active teacher support to handle dominance and negativity.
