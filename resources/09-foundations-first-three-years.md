# Foundations: The First Three Years

### A practical, do-today guide for parents and carers
#### Birth to three. Part of the Human-Core Initiative.

The first three years build the platform that everything else stands on. The brain grows faster now than it ever will again, language switches on, the body learns to move, and the very first self-control appears. You cannot drill a baby, and you should not try. What you can do is give the developing brain the handful of inputs it is built to use, and protect those inputs from the few things that crowd them out.

This guide is the concrete version. For each stage it tells you what is developing, the few things that matter most with specific activities to do, what to protect against, and how to tell it is working. It needs no apps, no flashcards, no special toys, and no money. It needs you, present and responsive, more than anything you can buy.

**How to use it.** Find your child's stage, read the short "do this" list, and pick two or three things to start this week. The ages are rough guides, not deadlines. Children vary, and the order matters more than the timing.

---

## The five things that matter most

These five run through every stage below. If you remember nothing else, remember these.

1. **Serve and return.** Your baby acts, you respond, they respond back. A coo answered, a point followed, a babble taken seriously as if it were speech. This back-and-forth is the single most important thing that builds a young brain. You are not teaching, you are having a relationship, and the relationship is the curriculum.

2. **Talk in turns, do not just talk at them.** It is not the number of words a child hears that builds language, it is the number of back-and-forth conversational turns. So pause. Leave a gap. Treat every sound they make as their turn to speak, and answer it.

3. **Let the body move.** Floor time, tummy time, crawling, climbing, walking, falling and getting up. Movement at this age is not exercise, it is brain building. Free, safe space to move beats any container or device.

4. **Guided play and real exploration.** Let the child lead, and gently add to what they are already doing. Narrate it, extend it, ask "what happens if?" Real objects, real world, real mess. They learn by doing, not by watching.

5. **Rhythm, sleep, and calm.** Predictable routines, fiercely protected sleep, and an adult who stays calm when the child cannot. Big feelings are not misbehaviour at this age, they are a nervous system that has not finished being built. You are the child's borrowed self-control until their own arrives.

And the one thing to protect all five from: **screens.** For the youngest children, time on a screen is mostly time stolen from the five things above. Keep the baby and toddler world analog, social, and physical. (More on this, and what changes as they grow, in The Problem With Screens.)

## Birth to 6 months

**What is developing.** The senses, the bond with you, head and neck control, the first sounds, the social smile. The baby is learning the most basic lesson of all: the world responds to me, and I am safe.

**Do this.**
- **Have "conversations" now, before words.** When your baby coos or gurgles, answer as if they said something, then wait. Watch them light up and take another turn. This is the beginning of language.
- **Get face to face.** Babies are built to study faces. Hold them close, make expressions, let them stare. Name what you are both looking at.
- **Tummy time, a little and often.** Short sessions on the floor on their front, building up as they get stronger. It develops the neck, back, and arms they will need to crawl.
- **Respond to cries.** You cannot spoil a baby this age. Reliably comforting them is how they learn the world is safe, which is the foundation for everything brave they will do later.
- **Narrate and sing.** Talk through your day out loud, and sing. Rhythm and the music of your voice feed the language system.

**Protect against.** Screens (no screen time at this age beyond video calls with family). Long stretches strapped in seats, swings, and bouncers, which limit movement. Anything that gets between you and responsive, in-person attention.

**Signs it is working.** They track your face, calm to your voice, coo and "answer" you, smile socially, and gain head control. None of this is a race. Slow and steady is fine.

## 6 to 12 months

**What is developing.** Sitting, then crawling. Babbling that starts to sound like talking. The discovery that objects still exist when hidden. Pointing, reaching, and the beginning of "I want that."

**Do this.**
- **Give them the floor.** Safe, open floor space to roll, sit, and crawl is worth more than any toy. Let them work to reach things; the struggle is the point.
- **Play peekaboo and hiding games.** Hiding a toy under a cloth and finding it teaches that things still exist when out of sight, and it is turn-taking practice too.
- **Talk about what they look at.** Follow their gaze and their reach, name the thing, and pause. Treat their babble as conversation and reply.
- **Offer real objects to explore.** Things to bang, drop, stack, and put in and out of a container. Dropping a spoon fifty times is a baby running a physics experiment, not being annoying.
- **Read board books together.** Let them hold the book, chew it, turn the pages. Point and name. Short and often beats long and forced.
- **Let them feed themselves.** Safe finger foods build the fine motor control and independence that handwriting and tools will later need.

**Protect against.** Baby walkers (they can actually delay walking and are a safety risk; a child learns to walk by pulling up and cruising). Screens. Doing everything for them before they have tried.

**Signs it is working.** Back-and-forth babbling, sitting and crawling, looking for a hidden toy, pointing, and bringing things to show you.

## 12 to 18 months

**What is developing.** First words. Walking, then climbing. Pointing to share ("look at that!"), not just to demand. Copying everything you do.

**Do this.**
- **Narrate and expand.** When they say "ball," you say "yes, a big red ball, let's roll the ball." You are stretching one word into language.
- **Follow their point.** When they point, look, name it, and talk about it. Shared attention on the same thing is where vocabulary grows fastest.
- **Walk, climb, carry, and explore outdoors.** Let them toddle, clamber, and haul things about. Childproof the space so the answer can usually be "yes, explore" rather than "no."
- **Play in and out.** Posting shapes through a hole, filling and emptying containers, stacking and knocking down. Simple, repetitive, and exactly right.
- **Name the world and the body.** Point to and name body parts, objects, and people. Turn it into a game.
- **Build small routines.** "First shoes, then park." Predictable sequences help them feel safe and start to understand time.

**Protect against.** Screens as a default soother. Finishing tasks for them too quickly. A home so full of "no" that exploring feels dangerous.

**Signs it is working.** A handful of first words, walking, pointing to share things with you, copying you, and following a simple instruction like "give it to Daddy."

## 18 to 24 months

**What is developing.** A word explosion and the first two-word phrases. The start of pretend play. Running. And a strong new sense of self, which arrives with the word "no" and with tantrums. The tantrums are not a fault, they are a small person with big feelings and no brakes yet.

**Do this.**
- **Play pretend.** Feed the teddy, "talk" on a toy phone, put dolly to bed. Pretend play is rehearsal for thinking, language, and getting along with others.
- **Expand their phrases.** "Dog bark" becomes "yes, the dog is barking loudly." Keep adding a little more language than they gave you.
- **Sing songs with actions.** Rhymes and action songs build language, memory, rhythm, and coordination all at once.
- **Climb, balance, and run.** Low walls to balance along, steps to climb, space to run. Build the balance and coordination that come before everything athletic.
- **Sort and match in real life.** Sorting socks by colour, putting the big ones here and the small ones there. Early maths is hidden in the laundry.
- **Name feelings out loud.** "You are frustrated because the tower fell." Naming the feeling is the first brick of self-control. Stay calm and be their calm; do not punish the feeling.

**Protect against.** Screens (still keep them minimal). Treating tantrums as defiance to be punished rather than feelings to be coached. Over-scheduling that leaves no time to just potter.

**Signs it is working.** Fifty or more words and starting to put two together, simple pretend play, running, and pointing to pictures in a book when you name them.

## 2 to 3 years

**What is developing.** Real sentences. A flood of "why?" Elaborate pretend stories. Strong emotions and the first, wobbly attempts to manage them. Jumping, and far better hands.

**Do this.**
- **Have real conversations.** Ask questions, wait for the answer, and ask another. Follow the "why" as far as you can. The back-and-forth still matters more than anything.
- **Feed the pretend play.** Build dens, run a pretend shop, act out the day's events. Give it time and props and join in when invited.
- **Read and ask what happens next.** Pause in a familiar story and let them tell you. Talk about the pictures and the feelings of the characters.
- **Count real things, one at a time.** Count the stairs as you climb, the grapes on the plate. Number is about quantity in the real world, not symbols on a page.
- **Play with the sounds in words.** Rhymes, silly songs, "what else starts like ball, bat, bus?" This sound play is the bridge to reading later, built years early and entirely by ear.
- **Build the hands.** Crayons, chunky puzzles, threading big beads, child-safe scissors with help, playdough. This is the groundwork for writing.
- **Play simple turn-taking games.** Rolling a ball back and forth, a very simple board game, "your turn, my turn." Waiting for a turn is self-control in training.
- **Involve them in real work.** Stirring, sweeping, carrying, watering. Real, useful tasks beat most toys for attention, pride, and skill.

**Protect against.** Screens still take the place of better things; keep them minimal, and watch alongside if at all. The temptation to start formal academics: a two-year-old does not need flashcards or worksheets, they need play, talk, and movement. Lost sleep and lost outdoor time.

**Signs it is working.** Short sentences and lots of questions, rich pretend play, following a two-step instruction, counting a few objects meaningfully, and enjoying rhymes.

## When to ask for help

This guide describes the usual path, but children develop at different rates, and some need extra support that is most effective when it starts early. Trust your instincts and ask a professional (your doctor, health visitor, or a speech and language therapist) if, for example, your child is not babbling or gesturing by around their first birthday, has no words by around 18 months, is not putting words together by around two and a half, loses skills they previously had, or does not respond to their name or make eye contact.

Asking is never an overreaction. Early support for speech, language, hearing, and development is a well-established good. This is the kind of supported, professional screening the framework backs. It is the opposite of the line we will not cross: we never build tools that try to infer a child's feelings or inner state. Judgment about a child belongs to the people who love them and the professionals who help them, not to an algorithm.

## Why we are confident this works

This guide is built on settled findings, not fashion.

- Responsive, back-and-forth interaction (serve and return) is the central driver of early brain development, and the architecture of the brain is built in sequence, the simple before the complex (Harvard Center on the Developing Child).
- The number of back-and-forth conversational turns predicts children's language and brain development far better than the sheer number of words they hear (Romeo et al., 2018).
- Young children's executive functions, the self-control that predicts later success, can be supported through play, movement, and routine (Diamond and Lee, 2011, *Science*).
- Guided play matches or beats both formal instruction and unstructured free time for learning at this age (Skene et al., 2022).
- Fundamental movement skills are the foundation of later physical competence and are linked to the same executive functions that underpin learning.
- For the youngest children, screen media largely displaces these inputs, which is why pediatric guidance advises against screen time other than video chat in the first roughly eighteen months.

Full references and the wider method are in the MYCELIUM Learning Framework, the curriculum this guide belongs to. This is the first practical, stage-by-stage module; the building years and beyond follow the same format.

## The one rule

You do not need to be perfect, buy anything, or turn your home into a classroom. Be present, respond, talk in turns, let them move and play, protect their sleep, and keep the screens away. The rest takes care of itself. The most powerful thing in a young child's world is an ordinary adult who pays attention.
