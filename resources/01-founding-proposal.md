# The Human-Core Initiative

### An open-source framework for human development in the age of AI
#### Founding proposal, version 0.2

---

## The problem, in numbers

There are two crises, and they compound.

The first is that children are not learning the foundations, at a scale most people do not register. The World Bank estimates that around seven in ten 10-year-olds in low- and middle-income countries cannot read and understand a simple story, a figure that rose from 57% to roughly 70% through the pandemic. In many countries, after three years of schooling, the large majority of children still cannot reliably read simple words. Wealthy systems are not exempt. Their absolute levels are higher, but international assessments show foundational skills in reading and mathematics declining across many developed countries too.

The cause is not mainly a mystery, and it matches what any honest observer sees in classrooms. Teaching is too often unresponsive. A well-documented pattern is that teachers teach to the top of the class, the few children already at the level of the curriculum, while most of the class is left behind. In early-years settings the adult is frequently passive, sometimes harsh, and a child who shows distress is read as a problem to manage rather than a person to understand. None of this is the fault of individual teachers. They are given almost no usable feedback on their own practice, little coaching, and no time, then asked to track twenty or thirty developing minds at once.

The second crisis is that AI now does most of what the old system trained children for, while also giving them a frictionless way to avoid the thinking that builds their minds. A system that was already failing to develop children is now also obsolete in its aims and newly easy to cheat.

The combined effect is a generation at risk of being neither well-developed as humans nor prepared for the world they will actually inhabit. The economic framing alone is stark, with the lost lifetime earnings of this cohort estimated in the trillions. The human framing is worse.

This initiative exists to act on both crises at once, in the open, globally, rather than to complain about them.

---

## What already exists, and why this is still needed

Honesty about the landscape is a condition of credibility. A great deal of good work already exists, and this project builds on it rather than ignoring it.

- The science of learning has produced a solid, if underused, consensus on what works: explicit instruction for novices, knowledge before skill, retrieval and spaced practice, and the central role of teacher quality.
- Targeted instruction, especially the Teaching at the Right Level model developed by Pratham and evaluated by J-PAL, is among the most cost-effective interventions ever measured and has reached tens of millions of children.
- Bodies like the Education Endowment Foundation and the World Bank's evidence panels already synthesize and rank what works.
- Teacher coaching is proven to lift instruction substantially, and early AI tools that give teachers feedback on their classroom discourse already exist.
- The science of reading has settled much of the debate on early literacy.

So what is missing, and why build anything at all?

Three things. First, none of these efforts is designed for the AI inflection point. They do not teach children to master and audit AI, and they do not use AI to scale the teacher coaching that has never reached most teachers. Second, they are fragmented. The pedagogy, the teacher development, and the technology live in separate worlds, separate organizations, separate paywalls. Third, very little of it is genuinely open, free, and forkable in a way a single teacher or parent anywhere can pick up and adapt.

The Human-Core Initiative is not a new pedagogy invented from scratch. That would be arrogant and wasteful. It is a free, open synthesis of what already works, extended with the one dimension the existing efforts predate: the arrival of capable AI, pointed deliberately at empowerment rather than surveillance.

## What we believe

These principles are constitutional. They are the test every contribution must pass. Each one is a short rule you can hold in your head and use to judge a decision.

**Build the human before the skill.** Development rests on sleep, movement, play, language, and a present adult who talks and listens. These are the foundation, not the extras, and they come first.

**The child does the thinking.** Learning is the residue of the child's own mental effort. Any tool, screen, adult, or AI that does the thinking for the child is removed, however impressive.

**Knowledge before skill.** Critical thinking, creativity, and judgment grow from a deep base of knowledge. Build the base, and the higher abilities become possible.

**Meet the child where they are.** Teach at the child's actual level, not the level of the curriculum and not the top of the class. Move them forward from there.

**Hard but achievable.** Guide the child's effort at the edge of what they can do. Effortful success is where learning and curiosity live; boredom and defeat are where they die.

**Take what works, from anywhere.** We build on the strongest global evidence about how humans actually learn, and we drop what is merely fashionable or familiar, however beloved.

Two commitments hold alongside these principles. The framework is **free and open** forever, owned by no company and forkable by anyone. And we hold one hard **boundary**, stated in full below: our tools serve the human, and we never build systems that infer the emotional or inner states of children.

## The three pillars

### Pillar 1. An open, evidence-based pedagogy

A complete, age-banded curriculum and methods guide that any teacher or parent can adopt without permission or payment. It synthesizes the proven foundations into one principle expressed in three developmental forms: an adult guides the child's mental effort toward a clear goal, at a difficulty that is hard but achievable, starting from the child's actual level.

- **Foundations, birth to six.** Guided play, rich language, movement, and self-regulation. At this age guided play matches or beats formal instruction, and structured neglect loses to both.
- **Building, six to ten.** The explicit-instruction years. Systematic decoding for reading, concrete-to-pictorial-to-abstract mathematics, direct teaching of science concepts that confronts misconceptions, daily retrieval and spaced practice, and grouping by actual level so the child who is behind is taught where they are.
- **Deepening, ten to twelve.** Knowledge is applied through projects, structured inquiry, teaching younger peers, and the AI-mastery work of Pillar 3.

Beneath all three: sleep, movement, play, and a present adult who talks and listens.

### Pillar 2. AI that empowers teachers

The reframing of the original vision, and stronger than it. An AI in the room can give a teacher understanding they would never get otherwise. The correction is the target. It listens to the teaching, not to the children's feelings.

What it does:
- **Gives every teacher a coach.** It analyzes the lesson's patterns, teacher versus child talk, question types, whether the teacher built on what children said, wait time, and reflects this back as private formative feedback. This turns the one proven transformative intervention, coaching, from a scarce luxury into something every teacher can have.
- **Supports human observation of development.** It helps a teacher structure and remember what they observe about each child's language, motor, and social milestones, so the struggling child is noticed early rather than mislabeled. Judgment stays human.
- **Flags developmental and language needs early.** Supported screening for communication, speech, and language delay is a legitimate, medically grounded use, distinct from emotion inference, handled with clinical safeguards.

What it must never do, by constitution: infer a child's emotion or inner state; score, rank, or profile children; or replace the teacher's relationship with the child.

One critical design constraint: the tool is owned by the teacher and private to the teacher. The moment it becomes a dashboard for administrators to monitor staff, it stops being coaching and becomes surveillance of teachers, and teachers will rightly reject it. Its data belongs to the person being developed, not to the institution.

### Pillar 3. AI mastery for children

Children will live and work inside AI for life. The goal is to make them its masters and editors, not its passive consumers. Built for the deepening years, it teaches children to use AI as a thinking partner they control, to audit its output for hallucination and bias, to understand how it works and fails, and to reason about its ethics. This is the pillar where the founder's expertise in auditing AI is an unfair advantage, and where the open market gap is largest.

## The boundary, stated once and globally

The initiative draws one hard line, and it holds everywhere, not only under European law. We do not build systems that infer the emotional or inner states of children. The reason is not merely regulatory. It is that such inference is scientifically unreliable, context-dependent, and aimed at people who cannot refuse it. Everything the original vision wanted from it, understanding the struggling child and helping the adult respond, is delivered better by pointing the technology at the teaching and at development. We lose nothing real and gain a foundation that survives scrutiny anywhere.

## Theory of change, and how we will know it works

The logic is explicit so it can be tested and falsified.

If teachers receive private, frequent feedback on their own practice (Pillar 2), their teaching becomes more responsive. If the pedagogy is evidence-based and meets each child at their level (Pillar 1), foundational learning improves, especially for the children furthest behind. If children learn to think with and audit AI (Pillar 3), they are prepared for the actual world rather than displaced by it. The three together aim at one outcome: children who are both well-developed as humans and capable in an AI world.

We will not claim success from enthusiasm. The measures are concrete and modest:
- For Pillar 1: gains in foundational reading and numeracy, measured simply and frequently, with particular attention to the lowest-performing children, since narrowing that gap is the real test.
- For Pillar 2: observable change in teacher practice, for example the shift from teacher-dominated talk toward children reasoning aloud, and teachers' own reported usefulness.
- For Pillar 3: children demonstrating that they can catch an AI's errors and explain their own work, not merely produce it.

We will publish what fails as openly as what works. A framework that hides its null results is not evidence-based.

## Strategy: prove locally, design globally

Global ambition does not mean starting everywhere. It means starting where there is trust and access, proving the pieces, and building everything to be translated and forked from day one.

The beachhead is the founder's own region and network, where established relationships with chambers of commerce, research parks, universities, and adult-education and care organizations provide real adoption channels and a credible research partner to validate the pedagogy. The first pilots run there, small and well-observed, because the evidence is clear that coaching and instruction programs lose much of their effect when scaled carelessly. We earn scale by proving the model works small, then packaging it so others can run it without us.

From that base, the framework is built multilingual and context-flexible, so it serves both the wealthy-world problem of AI disruption and the global-majority problem of foundational learning, which are converging faster than most assume.

## Risks, honestly

A founding document that names no risks is not serious.

- **Adoption is the hardest part, not the building.** Teachers are overloaded and rightly skeptical of new initiatives. Mitigation: make the first step tiny and immediately useful, start with volunteers, never mandate.
- **Open-source education projects often die from neglect.** Mitigation: secure sustaining funding before scaling, and keep the core small enough to maintain.
- **Effects shrink at scale.** Proven in the coaching literature. Mitigation: resist premature scaling, protect implementation quality over reach.
- **The teacher tool could become teacher surveillance.** Mitigation: constitutional rule that the data belongs to the teacher, not the institution.
- **AI tools can be inaccurate and erode trust.** Early teacher-feedback tools already show this, where perceived inaccuracy reduces uptake. Mitigation: position AI as a mirror prompting reflection, never as an authority, and keep the human in charge of every judgment.
- **Grandiosity.** The vision can outrun the evidence and discredit itself. Mitigation: understated claims, published failures, and the discipline of letting the work speak.

## Governance and openness

- **License.** Curriculum and documentation under Creative Commons Attribution-ShareAlike. Software under a permissive open-source license. Free forever, derivatives kept open.
- **Constitution.** The principles above, including the boundary, cannot be overridden by contribution.
- **Evidence standard.** Claims cite their evidence. Critique that strengthens the base is a valued contribution.
- **Multilingual from the start.** Founding work in English for reach, German and Italian as first translations, open invitation to localize anywhere.
- **Research partnership.** A research institution validates and stress-tests the pedagogy, so it is more than one practitioner's conviction.

---

## Sustainability

The framework is free and stays free, funded so it never charges the schools and families who use it.

- **Founder seed.** A committed share of the profits from the founder's existing software venture, dedicated to the core work. The signal of seriousness that unlocks the rest.
- **Grants.** Open educational resources, foundational literacy and numeracy, and digital education are well-funded across the EU, Erasmus+, regional bodies, and global foundations, several of which already fund the evidence-based programs this builds on.
- **Institutional partners** for credibility, validation, and adoption.
- **Optional paid implementation.** Organizations wanting hands-on adoption help can pay for training and support, which funds the free core. The core is never gated.

---

## First moves

1. Finalize the Pillar 1 curriculum spine and put it in front of one practitioner and one research reader to find where it breaks.
2. Build Pillar 3, the children's AI-mastery module, as the first fully written and pilotable unit, because it is the most differentiated and most defensible.
3. Scope Pillar 2 as an open reference design for private, teacher-owned discourse feedback, building on tools and trials that already exist.
4. Stand up the repository, constitution, license, and contribution guide.
5. Open one grant conversation and one research-partner conversation in parallel.

We do not build the whole system first. We prove one piece, in the open, with the evidence on the table, and invite the world to build the rest.

### Evidence this proposal draws on

- World Bank, UNESCO, UNICEF and partners, *State of Global Learning Poverty* (2022 update and subsequent briefs), on the scale of the global foundational-learning crisis.
- Pratham and J-PAL on Teaching at the Right Level and targeted instruction as a highly cost-effective, scaled intervention, and on the tendency of teachers to teach to the top of the class.
- Kraft, Blazar and Hogan (2018) on the large effect of teacher coaching and the challenge of scaling it.
- Research on automated classroom-discourse feedback (TalkMoves; the Teacher Talk Tool; Stanford and Utah trials, 2024 to 2025).
- Kirschner, Sweller and Clark (2006) and Project Follow Through on guided and explicit instruction for novices.
- Skene et al. (2022) on guided play in early childhood.
- Willingham, *Why Don't Students Like School?* on knowledge preceding skill.
- The science of reading on explicit decoding instruction.
- Kestin et al. (2025) and the LearnLM schools trial (2025) on well-designed AI tutoring, with Kosmyna et al. (2025) on the harms of AI over-reliance.
- The EU AI Act, Article 5 and Recital 44, on the prohibition and the scientific unreliability of emotion inference in education.
