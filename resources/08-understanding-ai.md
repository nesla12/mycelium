# Understanding AI

### What it is, why it changes childhood, and why mastery beats banning
#### Part of the Human-Core Initiative

You do not need to be technical to make good decisions about AI and children. You need a clear picture of what these systems actually are, what they do to a developing mind, and why the obvious responses, banning it or surrendering to it, are both wrong.

---

## What AI actually is, in plain words

A modern AI language model is a system that has read an enormous amount of human writing and learned to predict the most likely next piece of text. That is the core of it. It is extraordinarily good at sounding right.

Three things follow from that, and they matter for every decision below:

- **It does not know or understand the way a person does.** It is predicting likely words, not checking what is true. It does not know you.
- **It can be confidently wrong.** It will invent facts, quotes, and whole sources, all sounding completely real. This is called hallucination, and it is not a glitch to be patched. It is a direct consequence of how the system works.
- **It carries slants.** It learned from a vast pile of human writing in which some voices are loud, some quiet, and some missing, so it absorbs biases that no one deliberately put there.

The newest systems also "reason" through steps before answering and are markedly more capable, beating human experts on some hard tests. They also still fail, sometimes on tasks a careful child would get right, and it is getting harder to predict when. Power and unreliability have grown together. So the skill is never to be impressed or dismissive, but to judge, case by case, whether to trust a particular answer.

---

## Why this changes childhood specifically

For the first time, a child can obtain the answer, the essay, the solved problem, without doing the thinking. And the thinking is the thing that builds the mind. Learning is the residue of mental effort: a child grows by struggling productively, and an AI offers a frictionless way to skip exactly that struggle.

This is the core danger, and it has a name: **cognitive offloading**. The evidence points one way. Using AI as a substitute for your own thinking measurably weakens that thinking over time. Using it to push your thinking further can strengthen it. The mind is like a muscle the machine can either replace or train, depending entirely on how it is used. The trap is that offloading feels great in the moment and charges you the cost later, quietly, where you cannot see it.

A 2025 MIT Media Lab study put this on a brain scan. People wrote essays either with an AI assistant, with a search engine, or with only their own minds, while their brain activity was recorded. The AI group showed the weakest brain connectivity and engagement, produced the most uniform and similar essays, and, strikingly, many could not quote or recall what they had just submitted moments earlier. The researchers named what builds up "cognitive debt" (Kosmyna et al., 2025). It is a single early study, but it makes the mechanism vivid: outsource the thinking, and the thinking simply does not happen.

So the governing test, which never changes: **if the AI increases how much the child thinks, it is a tutor. If it replaces the child's thinking, it is a crutch that quietly weakens them.**

---

## The false choice

Faced with this, schools and families tend to reach for one of two responses, and both fail.

- **Ban it.** The child is left unprepared for a world built on these tools, and uses them anyway, in secret, with no guidance and no judgment.
- **Surrender to it.** The child offloads the thinking, hands in work they cannot explain, and is quietly weakened by the very tool that was supposed to help.

There is a third way, and it is the only one that works.

---

## The third way: mastery

Teach children to use AI, doubt it, and stay in charge of their own minds. Concretely, a child who has mastered AI can:

- explain what it is doing without saying it "thinks" or "knows";
- do their own thinking first, then use the tool to check, challenge, and extend;
- catch its fabrications using their own knowledge, and verify what they do not know against real sources;
- notice its slants and missing perspectives;
- turn a draft into work they can defend line by line;
- and reason about when using it is honest and when it is not.

This is not advanced or technical. It is a teachable set of habits, and it rests on something built earlier: a child with real knowledge, built attention, and the habit of their own effort is a child who can judge a machine. That is why this framework protects the foundations first and adds AI mastery from about age ten, once the child has the knowledge to judge what a machine tells them. Younger children are not ready for this; their job is to build the knowledge first.

---

## Where the line is

We point AI at empowering the adults and at teaching children, and never at the children themselves. By constitution, this initiative does not build or endorse systems that infer the emotional or inner states of children. The tools serve the human. That boundary holds everywhere, and it is the project's signature, not a footnote.

## Sources and grounding

This document synthesises the current picture of capable AI and the evidence on how its use affects learning, with the key sources checked against their originals.

- The cognitive-science principle that learning is the residue of the learner's own thinking, and that knowledge precedes the ability to evaluate critically.
- Kosmyna et al. (2025), *Your Brain on ChatGPT: Accumulation of Cognitive Debt When Using an AI Assistant for Essay Writing Task* (MIT Media Lab), a small brain-activity study on the cost of offloading writing to an LLM. Early evidence, but pointed.
- Kestin et al. (2025) and the LearnLM schools trial (2025) on well-designed AI tutoring that supports rather than replaces thinking.
- The state of capable AI in this period: reasoning models with extended chains of thought; systems that exceed human experts on some benchmarks while still failing a meaningful share of real tasks; and the persistence of hallucination and bias as consequences of how the systems work.
- The UNESCO AI Competency Framework for Students (2024), to which the Pillar 3 teaching is aligned.
