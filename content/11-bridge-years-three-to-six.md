# The Bridge Years: Ages Three to Six

### From play to the doorway of school
#### The precursors that matter, and how to build them without drilling. Part of the Human-Core Initiative.

These years are a bridge. The child is still firmly in the world of play, and should be, but this is where the specific precursors to reading and mathematics are quietly laid, and where the first short bursts of real teaching begin to fit alongside the play. Get this band right and the building years that follow have something to build on.

There are two equal and opposite ways to get it wrong. One is to push formal schooling too early, with worksheets, flashcards, and drilling, which the evidence does not support and which can dent a child's motivation before school has even begun. The other is to assume it all happens by itself and leave the precursors to chance. The path between them is **intentional, playful building** of the few things that genuinely predict how a child will do later.

---

## What school readiness actually is

School readiness is not knowing the alphabet and counting to twenty by rote. A child can chant letters and numbers and still arrive unready, and a child who cannot do either can arrive well prepared.

Real readiness is made of: **oral language**, **self-regulation** (the ability to wait, focus, and manage feelings), **fine and gross motor control**, **curiosity and a love of finding things out**, and a small set of **specific early-literacy and number precursors**. The large research review by the National Early Literacy Panel (2008) found the strongest predictors of later reading to be things like a child's knowledge of letters, their awareness of the sounds in words, how quickly they can name familiar letters and objects, and their ability to write their own name. Those are the targets. The rest of this guide is how to build them, almost entirely through play and talk.

## The five things to build

Underneath all five sits the thing that matters most of all at this age, **self-regulation**, the executive functions that let a child hold a goal, wait a turn, and stick with something. It is built the same way it was in the early years: games with rules, pretend play with a plan, turn-taking, and a calm adult who lends their own self-control until the child grows their own. It predicts school success as well as early academics do, and it makes all five of the following possible.

### 1. Oral language and storytelling

Still the single richest thing you can do. Keep talking in turns, the real back-and-forth, not a stream of words at them. Now stretch it: introduce new and interesting words and use them again; ask "why" and "what do you think will happen"; and above all, build **narrative**. Tell stories, retell the day, let the child tell you what happened. A child who can tell a clear story is a child whose mind is becoming organised for everything that follows.

When you read together, do not just read. Talk about the book: ask questions, let them predict, connect it to their life, let them "read" it back to you. This kind of shared, conversational reading is one of the highest-value things in the whole band.

### 2. The sounds inside words

This is the bridge into reading, and it is built entirely by ear, with no letters needed at first. Children's awareness of the sounds in language develops in a predictable order, from big units to small, and you teach it in that order through games:

1. **Whole words and syllables.** Clap the beats in names and words (*el-e-phant*). Break a sentence into words.
2. **Rhyme and alliteration.** Nursery rhymes, silly rhyming games, "what else starts like *sun, sock, sausage*?" Rhyme is the friendly front door to sound awareness.
3. **Individual sounds (phonemes), the hardest and most important.** "What sound does *cat* start with?" Then blending sounds into a word (*c-a-t... cat!*) and pulling them apart. This beginning phoneme awareness, arriving around five or six, is what makes learning to read click.

The goal across this band is to send a child toward formal reading with rhyme mastered, the first sounds in words audible to them, and a beginning ability to blend two or three sounds into a word.

### 3. Letters and print

Letter knowledge is one of the strongest single predictors of later reading, so build it, but playfully and through meaning, never by drilling.

- Teach **letter sounds as well as names**, because the sounds are what reading runs on. Start with the letters in the child's own name and the high-utility ones (*s, a, t, p, i, n*) rather than marching through the alphabet.
- Let them **write their name.** Name-writing is both a precursor in its own right and a source of real pride.
- Build **print concepts** through shared reading: that we read left to right and top to bottom, that the print (not the picture) carries the words, that a book has a front and a beginning. Run your finger under the words sometimes.
- Surround them with print in use: labels, lists, signs, their drawings captioned. Let them see that print does things.

### 4. Early number sense

Early maths at this age is about quantity in the real world, not symbols and sums on a page. The things that predict later mathematics are a feel for number, and you build them in daily life:

- **Counting that means something.** Counting with one item per number word (one-to-one), and understanding that the last number you say is how many there are (that "five" means the whole set, not just the last object).
- **Subitising.** Recognising small amounts (two, three, four) at a glance without counting, for example on dice and fingers.
- **Comparing.** More, fewer, the same. Bigger, smaller, longer, shorter. Sharing things out fairly.
- **Patterns and shape.** Making and continuing simple patterns (red, blue, red, blue), naming and noticing shapes.

Cook, build, sort, set the table, climb the stairs counting as you go. The maths is hidden in the day, and that is exactly where it should be.

### 5. The hand and the body

Handwriting later depends on hands that are ready now. Build them: drawing and scribbling and colouring, playdough and squeezing and pinching, threading beads, using child-safe scissors, doing up buttons and zips. A strong, controlled hand frees the mind to compose later. And gross motor is still enormous, climbing, balancing, running, throwing, and catching build the core strength, coordination, and attention that sitting and writing will need.

## The balance of play and teaching

The default at three to six is still **guided play**: play with a gentle learning goal the adult holds, while the child leads and explores inside it. Across this age, guided play matches or beats both unstructured free-for-all and formal direct instruction for learning (Skene et al., 2022).

Onto that, you add **short, playful, explicit bursts** of teaching as readiness appears: a few minutes on a letter sound, a rhyming game, counting a real collection. Keep these focused sessions genuinely short, five to fifteen minutes at most, and stop while it is still fun. Follow the child: if they are not ready for phonemes or letters, keep building oral language and sound play, and the readiness will come. There is no prize for being early, and real cost to forcing it.

A light week looks like this: reading aloud and talking every day, a daily rhyme or sound game, letters and numbers woven into play and daily life, plenty of movement and free play and outdoors, and perhaps one short focused activity a day. That is enough. The richest curriculum at this age is an interested adult, a lot of talk, and time to play.

## How to tell it is working

By around five or six, as a rough guide and never a race, you would hope to see a child who can:

- hold a back-and-forth conversation and tell a simple story in order;
- enjoy rhymes, hear the first sound in a word, and blend two or three sounds into a word;
- recognise many letters and write their own name;
- count a small set with one item per number, and say how many there are;
- recognise two, three, or four things at a glance;
- draw, hold a pencil with growing control, and use scissors;
- wait for a turn, separate from you calmly, and stay with a task for a while.

These are guides to notice gaps early, not gates to push children through. Children arrive at each one on their own timetable.

## What to avoid

- **Formal, academic schooling that crowds out play.** Worksheets, drilling, and long sitting at this age do not help and can harm a child's motivation. Play is not the opposite of learning here; it is the method.
- **Racing and comparing.** The child who reads at four has no lasting advantage over the one who reads at six. Resist it.
- **Screens as a default.** They still displace the talk, play, and movement that build everything above. Keep them minimal.
- **Treating three to six as "school, but smaller."** It is its own stage, with its own job.
- **Pushing letters and phonemes before the sound play and oral language are there.** Build the readiness first.

---

## When to ask for help

Trust your instincts and ask a professional (your doctor, health visitor, or a speech and language therapist) if a child's speech is hard to understand by around four, if they show no interest in sounds, rhymes, or print as they approach school, if there are persistent difficulties with attention, movement, or getting along with other children, or if they lose skills they had. Early support is a well-established good, and asking is never an overreaction.

This is supported, professional screening by the people who know and love the child. It is the opposite of the line the framework will not cross: we never build tools that try to infer a child's feelings or inner state.

## Why we are confident this works

- The National Early Literacy Panel (2008) review identified alphabet knowledge, phonological awareness, rapid naming, and name-writing among the strongest predictors of later reading, which is why this band targets them.
- Phonological skills develop in a predictable order, from words and syllables to rhyme to individual sounds, which is why we teach them in that order (the progression summarised by Reading Rockets and the wider literature).
- Guided play matches or beats both formal instruction and free play for learning at this age (Skene et al., 2022).
- Young children's executive functions, which predict school success, can be supported through play, games, and routine (Diamond and Lee, 2011, *Science*).
- Early number sense, counting principles, subitising, and magnitude comparison, predicts later mathematics, and is best built in real, everyday activity.

References were checked against their originals; where the evidence is contested or modest, we say so. This is the third practical module of the curriculum, sitting between Foundations: The First Three Years and The Building Years: Reading and Mathematics.
