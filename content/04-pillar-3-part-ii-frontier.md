# Pillar 3, Part II: The Frontier
## Advanced AI Mastery, Reasoning, and the Skills School Forgot

### A workbook for capable 12 to 14 year olds. Free to use, adapt, translate, and fork.
#### Part of the Human-Core Initiative. Follows the foundational Pillar 3 workbook.

---

# Before you start: how this module is different

The foundational workbook taught children to catch an AI lying and stay in charge of their own work. This one goes further, into the questions that do not have clean answers, the ones most schools avoid because they are uncomfortable or because the adults do not know the answer either.

That is the point. We treat these young people as capable of hard questions. On the genuinely contested ones in here, who should own a thought, who should control the world's intelligence, what the future holds, **the teacher does not supply the right answer, because there isn't one.** Your job is to make them reason, argue, change their minds, and defend a position they actually built. A child who can do that is harder to manipulate than one who memorized the correct opinion.

This module also takes a position the foundational one was too cautious to take: school misses some of the most important things in life, and AI is now powerful enough to help young people learn them anyway. Unit 7 makes the children prove or disprove that themselves.

**What stays non-negotiable, even here.** No personal data of any kind goes into an AI tool. Nothing is made about real, identifiable people, especially other children, without consent. The teacher supervises. These are not bureaucratic rules, they are how you protect children while teaching them to be bold. Boldness and carelessness are not the same thing.

**The thread running through all eight units:** AI is now genuinely powerful, genuinely fallible, and genuinely owned by a few. The educated person of this generation is the one who can use it, doubt it, and stay sovereign over their own mind while doing both.

# UNIT 1. How the machine actually thinks now

**The question.** It used to just predict the next word. Now it "reasons." What changed, and should that change how much we trust it?

**The real picture, told straight.** Today's strongest AI does not just blurt an answer. It generates a long chain of reasoning first, works through steps, and can catch some of its own mistakes. Spend more computing power while it thinks and it gets noticeably better. On some hard tests it now beats human experts, including PhD-level science and olympiad mathematics. And yet, set loose on real tasks in the real world, the best agents still fail something like one in three times, and it is getting harder, not easier, to see why they fail. Power and unreliability have grown together.

**What to say.** "It can now out-reason your teacher on a physics olympiad problem and also confidently mess up a task a careful ten-year-old would get right. Both are true at once. So the skill is not being impressed, and not being dismissive. It is judging, case by case, whether to trust this particular answer."

**Activity.** Give a reasoning model a hard multi-step problem and show its reasoning trace on the screen. The class hunts for the exact step where the logic slips, if it does. If you have no access, use a prepared flawed reasoning trace: a worked solution that reaches a wrong answer through a plausible-looking but broken middle step, and have them find the break. The skill is reading the reasoning, not just the answer.

**The durable skill.** Evaluating a chain of reasoning, not just a final answer. This is rarer and more valuable than knowing facts.

**Check.** Each student finds one flawed step in a piece of reasoning and explains why it fails.

**Pitfall.** The reasoning trace looks authoritative. Keep asking: does each step actually follow from the one before?

# UNIT 2. Compute is power, and it costs the Earth

**The question.** Intelligence can now be bought with computing power. Who should own that power?

**The real picture.** Modern AI gets smarter mainly by training on more data with more computing power, which costs enormous amounts of money and energy. Training a single frontier model can emit tens of thousands of tons of carbon. The result is that the ability to make intelligence is concentrated among the few organizations that can afford the compute. Whoever owns the compute owns a large share of the world's new intelligence. That is a fact about power, not just technology. There is a counter-current too: some labs release open models anyone can run, which spreads the power back out.

**What to say.** "Magic has a power bill. Every clever answer ran on machines in a building somewhere, using electricity and water, paid for by a company. So here is a real question with no settled answer: is it good or dangerous that the power to create intelligence sits with a handful of companies, and what would the alternative even look like?"

**Activity.** Research and debate. Students use the LLM to gather facts on what compute is, what it costs, who owns the biggest share, and what open models change. Then a structured debate: should the power to make intelligence be concentrated, regulated, or forced open? They must use real numbers and steelman the side they disagree with.

**The durable skill.** Seeing the infrastructure, money, energy, and power behind any technology that looks like magic. Follow the compute, follow the money, follow the energy.

**Check.** Each student states a position on compute and power and gives one fact and one counterargument.

**Pitfall.** This can collapse into "big companies bad." Push for the harder version: concentration also funds the research and the free tools they use. Make them hold the tension.

# UNIT 3. Who owns a thought? (Ownership on trial)

**The question.** When you make something with AI, who made it? And the rule from the foundational workbook, that you must be able to defend every line, is it even right?

**The real picture.** We put a rule on trial today, including one I taught you earlier. Humans have never created alone. We stand on language we did not invent, books we did not write, tools others built. A novelist uses an editor. A musician samples. A scientist builds on a thousand prior papers. So when is using AI like using a calculator, like having a co-author, like hiring a ghostwriter, and when is it simply passing off someone else's work as yours? The honest answer is that the line moves depending on what you claim and what you learned.

**What to say.** "I told you to own every line. Now attack that rule. Did Shakespeare own every word, when he borrowed plots and used a shared language? Is using AI to fix your grammar cheating? Is using it to write your whole essay cheating? Where exactly is the line, and who decides?"

**Activity, structured debate.** Pose escalating cases, from spell-check to full ghost-writing, and have students place each on a line from "totally fine" to "dishonest," defending their placement. Then they each write their own honest personal rule for AI use and authorship, in their own words, and defend it against challenge.

**The durable skill.** Thinking clearly about authorship, credit, and honesty in a world where almost everything is co-created. Forming a line you can actually defend.

**Check.** Each student articulates their own authorship rule and defends it against one hard case.

**Pitfall.** There is no class-wide correct answer here. Resist giving one. Reward the quality of reasoning, not the conclusion.

# UNIT 4. The end of "looking it up" (new research methods)

**The question.** When an AI can summarize anything in seconds, what does real research even mean now?

**The real picture.** "Look it up" is dead, because the lookup is instant and often wrong or fabricated. Real research now is a workflow: break the question into parts, use AI to map the landscape fast, then verify and triangulate against independent sources, dig down to primary sources, check provenance because fakes are everywhere, and finally synthesize your own view that goes beyond any single source. The machine does the gathering. The judging and the synthesis are yours, and that is the part that matters.

**What to say.** "Anyone can get an answer in three seconds now. That makes the answer worthless on its own. The valuable skill flipped. It used to be finding information. Now it is judging it and putting it together into something true that the AI did not just hand you."

**The modern research workflow, taught explicitly.**
1. Decompose the question into parts.
2. Map fast: use AI to get the landscape and the candidate sources quickly.
3. Verify and triangulate: confirm with independent sources that do not copy each other.
4. Go to the primary source: the original study, document, or data, not a summary of it.
5. Check provenance: where did this actually come from, and could it be fabricated?
6. Synthesize: write your own conclusion that no single source gave you.

**Activity.** Students run one real question they care about through all six steps and produce a one-page synthesis with sources, explicitly noting where the AI was wrong or shallow.

**The durable skill.** Modern research literacy: fast machine-assisted mapping followed by rigorous human verification and synthesis.

**Check.** Each student shows a synthesis that contains at least one insight not present in any single source they used.

**Pitfall.** They will want to stop at step 2. The whole value is in steps 3 to 6.

# UNIT 5. Thinking with a machine without losing your mind

**The question.** If the machine can think for you, when should you let it, and when will it make you weaker?

**The real picture.** This is the most important unit in the book. The evidence is clear that using AI to do your thinking for you measurably weakens your own thinking over time, while using it to push your thinking further can strengthen it. Your mind is like a muscle the machine can either replace or train, depending entirely on how you use it. The trap is that offloading feels great in the moment and costs you later. The skill is deciding, deliberately, when to use your own brain and when to reach for the tool.

**What to say.** "There will be a thousand moments a day when you could let it think for you. Some of those are smart. Some of those are you trading a bit of your own mind for convenience. Today you learn to feel the difference and to choose on purpose."

**Activity.** Two contrasting drills. First, offload on purpose: let AI solve something entirely, then notice that you cannot reconstruct it. Second, augment on purpose: think hard yourself, then use AI to attack your idea, find your blind spot, and push further than you could alone. Reflect on how different the two feel and what each costs and gives.

**The durable skill.** Cognitive self-control: choosing when to use your own mind, and noticing the moment you have quietly stopped thinking.

**Check.** Each student names a real situation where they will deliberately not use AI, with a reason about their own development.

**Pitfall.** This is abstract until they feel it. Make them experience the "I can't reconstruct it" moment.

# UNIT 6. The lone genius is a myth (collaboration)

**The question.** We just spent a unit on owning your own thinking. Now the opposite: almost nothing great was made alone. So what is real collaboration, with people and with machines?

**The real picture.** The individual-genius story is mostly a myth. Big things are made by groups, arguing, building on each other, dividing work. AI adds a strange new kind of teammate: tireless, fast, knowledgeable, and with no judgment, no stakes, and no accountability. Good collaboration with humans means listening, disagreeing well, and combining strengths. Good collaboration with AI means using it without dissolving your own contribution into it. The question that matters: in any team that includes AI, what did the humans uniquely bring?

**What to say.** "Forget the genius alone in a room. Real work is a team sport, and now one teammate is a machine. So when your group uses AI, the interesting question afterward is not what the AI did. It is what only the humans could have done."

**Activity.** A team challenge: groups solve a real, open problem, using AI as one teammate. Afterward they map who contributed what, and specifically name what was uniquely human, the judgment, the care, the choice of what mattered, the responsibility for the result.

**The durable skill.** Real collaboration with people, and knowing what humans bring that AI cannot: stakes, judgment, responsibility, and the choice of what is worth doing.

**Check.** Each group names three things in their work that only the humans could have provided.

**Pitfall.** Either the AI does everything or it is ignored. Steer toward genuine division of labor.

# UNIT 7. The ten skills school forgot

**The question.** What are the ten most important skills that regular Western schools usually fail to teach, and why?

**This is the heart of the module.** It is honest, it is slightly subversive, and it puts the children in charge of judging their own education.

**What to say.** "School teaches a lot. It also skips some of the most useful things in a whole life, sometimes because they are hard to test, sometimes because no one decided they were the school's job. Today you find out what is missing, and you argue about it, and then you start fixing it for yourselves."

**Activity, three phases.**
1. **Research.** In pairs, students use the LLM to investigate the question: what important life skills do schools commonly fail to teach? They gather candidates and the reasons given, and they verify rather than trust, because even this answer is just the AI's guess from its training data.
2. **Debate and build.** As a class, they argue toward their own ranked list of ten, with a reason for each. They must justify what makes the cut and what gets left off. Disagreement is the point.
3. **Act.** Each student picks one skill from the class list and makes a concrete plan to start learning it this term, using AI as a tutor where it helps. The module itself, learning to think with and against AI, is quietly one of the missing skills, and they should notice that.

**A seed bank for the teacher,** to spark the debate, not to dictate it. Offer these only if the class stalls, and invite them to cut and add freely: how to handle money and not get exploited by it; how to learn anything deliberately (learning how to learn); how to fail, recover, and try again; how to manage your own attention and emotions; how to negotiate, persuade, and say no; how to build relationships and ask for help; how to navigate institutions, contracts, and your own rights; how things actually work, practically, with your hands; how your body, sleep, and health work; how to start things and make them happen without permission; how to tell truth from manipulation; how to make a decision under uncertainty.

**The durable skill.** Agency over your own education: the ability to see what you are missing and go get it, without waiting for a school to provide it.

**Check.** Each student leaves with one missing skill chosen and a first concrete step planned.

**Pitfall.** Do not let your own list win. Let theirs win, even if it is different from yours. The ownership is the lesson.

# UNIT 8. Your move: betting on yourself in an uncertain future

**The question.** Nobody knows exactly where this goes. So what should you bet on becoming good at?

**The real picture, with honest uncertainty.** Capable people disagree sharply about the future, and anyone who gives you a confident date is guessing. What looks likely: AI agents in more and more of work and life, more tasks automated, truth harder to verify as fakes improve, and both new abundance and new risks. What that probably means for a human: routine knowledge work gets cheap, while judgment, taste, responsibility, the ability to decide what is worth doing, and the ability to work with and around AI get more valuable, not less. But this is a bet, not a prophecy.

**What to say.** "I am not going to tell you the future, because I do not know it and neither does anyone selling you a confident version of it. But you have to make choices now anyway. So we are going to reason about what is likely, and you are going to make your own bet on what to become good at, knowing you might be wrong and will adjust."

**Activity, scenario debate.** Pose a few plausible futures, optimistic, harsh, and weird. For each, the class works out: what would be more valuable for a human to be good at, and what less? Then each student writes a personal "durable bet": three things they will deliberately get good at because they are likely to stay valuable however the future breaks, and why.

**The durable skill.** Future literacy and agency: reasoning under uncertainty and steering your own development instead of being steered.

**Check.** Each student produces a personal durable bet with reasons, and one thing they will start now.

**Pitfall.** Avoid both doom and hype. Hold honest uncertainty, and keep returning the focus to what is in their control.

# Capstone project

Students take on a real problem they care about and produce something genuine, using everything in this module. They must research it properly (Unit 4), do the hard thinking themselves and use AI to push it further (Unit 5), collaborate (Unit 6), make something they can defend on their own honest authorship terms (Unit 3), and reflect on what was uniquely theirs. Judge the judgment and the reasoning, not the polish.

# For teachers and parents: why this is bold, not reckless

This module asks children to debate questions adults disagree on, and refuses to hand them tidy answers. That is deliberate. A young person who has reasoned their own way to a defensible position on ownership, power, or their own future is far more resilient, and far harder to manipulate, than one who memorized an approved view. The guardrails that remain, no personal data, supervision, consent, are about safety, not about controlling what children are allowed to think. We protect them fiercely and we trust their minds completely. Those two things go together.

# Evidence and grounding

- The 2026 state of AI: reasoning models with extended chains of thought and test-time compute scaling; agentic systems that act but still fail a significant share of real tasks; models exceeding human experts on some advanced benchmarks; the heavy compute, cost, and carbon behind all of it; and the shift from fake-detection to provenance as fakes outrun detectors.
- The evidence that AI used as a substitute for thinking reduces learning while AI used to extend thinking can improve it.
- The cognitive-science principle that knowledge precedes the ability to reason and evaluate, which is why this advanced module sits on top of the foundational one.
- The UNESCO AI Competency Framework for Students dimensions of human-centred mindset, ethics, techniques, and critical judgment, extended here toward genuine debate rather than compliance.
