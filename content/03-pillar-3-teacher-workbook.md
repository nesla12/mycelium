# Pillar 3: AI Mastery for Children
## The Complete Teacher Workbook

### Ages 10 to 12. Free to use, adapt, translate, and fork.
#### Part of the Human-Core Initiative. Aligned to the UNESCO AI Competency Framework for Students (2024).

---

# How to use this workbook

You are holding everything you need to teach children to use, question, and out-think AI. You do not need to be an AI expert. You do not need a budget. You do not even need reliable AI access in the room, because every session includes pre-made materials you can print and use offline.

**The shape.** Eight core sessions of 60 to 90 minutes, plus an optional capstone project. Run one per week across a term, or as an intensive block. Each session lists a 60-minute and a 90-minute runsheet.

**The age.** Ten to twelve. These children have enough knowledge to judge what a machine tells them, which is the whole point. Younger children are not ready for this; their job is to build the knowledge first.

**What you need.**
- This workbook.
- Ideally, one shared, school-approved AI tool the teacher operates on a projector, so no child needs an account. Check the tool's minimum age and your school's policy first.
- If you have no AI access at all, you can still run every session using the printed materials in Part B. The learning does not depend on live access.

**Your stance as the teacher.** Your job is not to know the answers. It is to model curiosity and healthy doubt. The single most powerful sentence in this whole workbook, which you will say constantly, is: *"How do we know that's true? Let's check."* When a child accepts an AI answer too fast, you do not correct them. You ask them how they know. The skepticism has to become theirs, not yours on loan.

**The one rule, on the wall all term.** *The child does the thinking first. AI checks, challenges, and extends, but never replaces your effort, and you never hand in work you cannot explain.*

# Standards alignment

This module maps to the four dimensions of the UNESCO AI Competency Framework for Students:
- **Human-centred mindset:** Sessions 2, 7, 8. The child stays in charge; the tool serves the human.
- **Ethics of AI:** Sessions 5, 8. Bias, honesty, privacy, consequences.
- **AI techniques and applications:** Sessions 1, 6. How it works, how to direct it.
- **Critical judgment, woven through all four:** Sessions 3, 4. Catching errors, verifying claims.

Using this workbook lets a school say, truthfully, that it teaches AI to a recognized international standard.

---

# Safety and guardrails (non-negotiable)

Post these and follow them in every session and any fork of this module.
- Children never type personal information, their own or anyone else's, into an AI tool.
- No AI is ever used to analyze, score, or guess the feelings or states of the children.
- No AI is used to make content about real, identifiable people without consent, and never about other children.
- The teacher operates or supervises all AI use.
- Children are encouraged to question AI, including the school's own AI tools. That questioning is the lesson. Welcome it.

# PART A. THE EIGHT SESSIONS

Each session lists: the objective, what to prepare, a timed runsheet, the exact things you can say, the activity, the formative check, how to support strugglers and stretch the fast, and the mistakes to expect. Handouts and worked materials referenced here are printable in Part B.

## Session 1. What is this thing, really?

**Objective.** Children can explain, in their own words, that an AI language model predicts likely text and does not know or understand, and therefore can be confidently wrong.

**Prepare.** Print Handout 1A. Optional: one AI tool on the projector.

**Runsheet (60 / 90 min).**
- Hook, 10 min. The prediction game (below).
- Input, 10 / 15 min. Explain prediction in plain words.
- Activity, 25 / 45 min. Watch AI generate; name what it does.
- Wrap, 15 / 20 min. Each child writes their one-sentence definition.

**The prediction game (hook).** Say the start of a familiar sentence and have children call out the next word.
- *"The cat sat on the ..."* (mat)
- *"Better late than ..."* (never)
- Then a trap: *"The astronaut opened the door and saw a ..."* Let them shout answers. Point out there is no single right answer, only likely ones, and that the most likely word can be wrong for this particular story.

**What to say (input).** *"An AI language model is a very powerful version of that game. It has read an enormous amount of text, and it guesses the next word, then the next, then the next. It is brilliant at sounding right. But it is guessing what is likely, not checking what is true. It does not know things the way you know your own name. And it does not know you."*

**Activity.** If you have AI access, give it a simple prompt on the projector and narrate: *"Watch. It is predicting. See how confident it sounds?"* If you have no access, read aloud the sample AI answer in Handout 1A and ask the children whether it sounds confident, and whether confidence is the same as being right.

**Formative check.** Each child completes the sentence on Handout 1A: *"An AI is doing this: ..."* without using the words "think" or "know." Collect. A good answer mentions predicting or guessing likely words.

**Support.** Give the sentence frame: *"An AI guesses the next ____ based on ____ it has read."*
**Stretch.** Ask: *"If it is only predicting likely words, why is it still useful?"*

**Pitfalls.** Children, and adults, naturally say the AI "knows" or "thinks." Gently catch this every time it happens, all term. The language shapes the trust.

## Session 2. Think first, then ask

**Objective.** Children experience why doing their own thinking before using AI makes them stronger, and can explain it.

**Prepare.** Choose one short task the class can do in 10 minutes: a maths problem, a paragraph to write, a question to answer. Print Handout 2A (the compare sheet).

**Runsheet.**
- Hook, 5 min. Pose the task. No AI yet.
- Own attempt, 15 min. Each child does it alone and keeps their work.
- AI attempt, 10 / 20 min. The same task goes to the AI (projector or printed sample).
- Compare, 20 / 40 min. Fill in Handout 2A in pairs.
- Wrap, 10 min. Share comparisons.

**What to say.** *"We are going to do this the hard way first, on purpose. You will try it yourself. Then we will ask the AI. Then we will compare. The interesting part is not the answer. It is what you notice when you compare."*

**Activity.** After their own attempt, show or read the AI's version. On Handout 2A, each pair writes: what the AI did better, what it got wrong or left out, and one thing they understood because they tried first.

**Formative check.** Each child names one thing they understand better because they attempted it before seeing the AI's answer.

**Support.** Pair a struggling child with a patient partner for the compare step.
**Stretch.** Ask: *"If you had only read the AI's answer and copied it, what would you not understand right now?"*

**Pitfalls.** Some children will resist doing it the hard way. Hold the line warmly. The whole module rests on this experience.

## Session 3. Catch the lie (hallucination)

**Objective.** Children find fabrications in AI output and understand that their own knowledge is the lie-detector.

**Prepare.** Print Handout 3A (the fabricated review) and Handout 3B (the hunt sheet).

**Runsheet.**
- Hook, 10 min. Define hallucination.
- Activity 1, 20 / 30 min. The made-up-book test.
- Activity 2, 20 / 40 min. The expert test on a topic they know.
- Wrap, 10 min. The rule.

**What to say.** *"AI sometimes invents things. Facts, quotes, even whole books and sources, all sounding completely real. This is called hallucination. Today you become detectives."*

**Activity 1, the made-up-book test.** Read Handout 3A aloud, an AI-style glowing review of a book that does not exist. Ask: *"How much of this is true?"* Lead them to the answer: none of the specifics can be true, because the book is invented, yet the AI described it with total confidence. This is hallucination in its purest form.

**Activity 2, the expert test.** Have the AI (or use a prepared printout) describe something the children are the experts on: your town, your school, a local event, the rules of a game they love. They hunt every error on Handout 3B. Because they are the experts, they need no answer key. You are not the authority here; they are.

**Formative check.** Each child marks at least one confident falsehood and explains how they knew.

**Support.** Start them on a topic they know extremely well so errors are obvious.
**Stretch.** Ask: *"Why is it more dangerous when the AI is wrong about something you don't already know?"* This sets up Session 4.

**Pitfalls.** Children may feel the AI is "broken." Reframe: it is not broken, this is how it works, which is exactly why we always check.

## Session 4. Verify

**Objective.** Children verify an unfamiliar AI claim against trustworthy sources.

**Prepare.** Print Handout 4A (three claims to check) and Handout 4B (the verify steps). Internet or reference books needed for at least part of this.

**Runsheet.**
- Hook, 10 min. AI answer as hypothesis, not conclusion.
- Teach, 10 / 15 min. The three verify moves.
- Activity, 30 / 55 min. Verify three claims.
- Wrap, 10 min. Show your sources.

**What to say.** *"In the last session you caught lies about things you knew. But what about things you don't know yet? Then you need a method. An AI answer is a starting guess, a hypothesis. It earns your trust only when you check it."*

**The three verify moves (teach explicitly).**
1. Find the original source. Who first said this, and can you find them?
2. Cross-check. Do two sources that do not copy each other agree?
3. Be suspicious of claims with no traceable origin, and of quotes and statistics with no source.

**Activity.** Using Handout 4A, children take three AI claims on a topic they do not already know and verify each with the three moves, recording their source on Handout 4B.

**Formative check.** Each child verifies one claim and can show the source that confirmed or contradicted it.

**Support.** Provide two pre-selected trustworthy sources so the search is not the obstacle.
**Stretch.** Give a claim that is subtly half-true and have them find exactly which part fails.

**Pitfalls.** Children may "verify" by asking the same AI again. Name this trap directly: asking the liar to confirm its own story is not checking.

## Session 5. The hidden slant (bias)

**Objective.** Children notice that AI output carries perspective and bias from its training data.

**Prepare.** Print Handout 5A (the two contrasting answers).

**Runsheet.**
- Hook, 10 min. Where does AI's "view" come from?
- Activity, 30 / 50 min. Compare and spot the slant.
- Discuss, 15 / 25 min. Whose voice, whose absence.
- Wrap, 5 min.

**What to say.** *"AI learned from a giant pile of human writing. Some voices are loud in that pile, some are quiet, some are missing. So the AI picks up slants without anyone deciding to put them there. Today we look for them."*

**Activity.** Use Handout 5A, two AI-style answers that reveal a slant, or generate live by asking the AI the same question about different people, places, or groups and comparing. Children mark: what is assumed, what is stereotyped, whose perspective is centered, whose is missing.

**Formative check.** Each child gives one concrete example of a slant or a missing perspective.

**Support.** Point to one specific line and ask: *"Did the AI just assume something here?"*
**Stretch.** Ask: *"If this AI helped decide something important, who might it treat unfairly, and why?"*

**Pitfalls.** Keep it about patterns in the text, handled calmly and age-appropriately, not about blaming or about heavy adult topics. The skill is noticing, not outrage.

## Session 6. Better questions (prompting as thinking)

**Objective.** Children improve a weak prompt into a strong one and see that clearer questions come from clearer thinking.

**Prepare.** Print Handout 6A (weak and strong prompt pairs) and Handout 6B (the prompt-improvement worksheet).

**Runsheet.**
- Hook, 10 min. A vague question gets a vague answer.
- Teach, 10 / 15 min. What makes a prompt strong.
- Activity, 30 / 55 min. Prompt battles in pairs.
- Wrap, 10 min. Before-and-after share.

**What to say.** *"If you ask a fuzzy question you get a fuzzy answer. A good prompt is just a clearly thought-out question. So getting better at prompting is really getting better at thinking clearly about what you actually want."*

**What makes a prompt strong (teach).** It says who it is for, what form you want, how long, and what to focus on. The most powerful move of all: ask the AI to argue the opposite case, or to critique its own answer.

**Activity, prompt battles.** In pairs, children take a weak prompt from Handout 6A and improve it step by step on Handout 6B, comparing the answers each version produces. Then they try the power move: *"Now argue the other side."*

**Formative check.** Each pair shows a before-and-after prompt and explains why the second is better.

**Support.** Give the four-part checklist (who, form, length, focus) as a fill-in frame.
**Stretch.** Challenge them to write a prompt that makes the AI find the weakness in its own first answer.

**Pitfalls.** Prompting can become a game of getting impressive output. Keep pulling it back to thinking: the point is the clarity in their own head, not tricks.

## Session 7. Make it yours (editing and ownership)

**Objective.** Children turn an AI draft into work they can defend line by line.

**Prepare.** Print Handout 7A (a mediocre AI draft to fix).

**Runsheet.**
- Hook, 5 min. The editor-in-chief idea.
- Activity, 35 / 60 min. Improve the draft.
- Defend, 15 / 20 min. Each child defends their version.
- Wrap, 5 min.

**What to say.** *"An AI can write a draft in seconds. That is the easy part, and it is not yours yet. Your job is to be the editor-in-chief: cut what is weak, fix what is wrong, add what it missed, put it in your own voice, and remove anything you cannot stand behind. When you are done, you must be able to defend every single line."*

**Activity.** Children take the deliberately weak AI draft in Handout 7A, full of vague, generic, repetitive lines and one shaky claim, and improve it using their own knowledge and voice. They mark what they cut, fixed, and added.

**Defend.** Each child reads one improved passage and answers: *"Why did you change that? How do you know your version is better or truer?"* If they cannot defend a line, it does not belong in their work.

**Formative check.** The child can defend their edits aloud with reasons.

**Support.** Ask them to find just three things to cut and one to add.
**Stretch.** Ask them to rewrite it for a completely different reader, for example a six-year-old.

**Pitfalls.** Some will lightly reword and call it done. Push for real ownership: could they have written this without the AI, and can they defend it?

## Session 8. Should you? (ethics)

**Objective.** Children reason about when using AI is honest and when it is not, and agree on shared norms.

**Prepare.** Print Handout 8A (the dilemmas) and the class agreement template in Part B.

**Runsheet.**
- Hook, 10 min. Can you versus should you.
- Discussion, 35 / 60 min. The dilemmas, in the Philosophy for Children style.
- Agreement, 15 / 20 min. Co-write the class AI agreement.

**What to say.** *"You can use AI for almost anything now. The harder question is when you should. Today there are no easy answers, and your job is to reason well and listen to each other."*

**Activity, structured discussion.** Pose the dilemmas from Handout 8A one at a time. Examples: Is it cheating to have AI write your homework? Is it cheating to have it check your homework? What should you never type into it? Who is affected when you use it, including people you cannot see and the planet? Use the discussion moves: ask for reasons, ask who disagrees, ask "what if" variations.

**Agreement.** Together, write a short class agreement on honest AI use, in the children's own words, using the template. Post it.

**Formative check.** Each child names one situation where they would use AI and one where they would not, each with a reason.

**Support.** Offer a sentence frame: *"Using AI is fair when ... and unfair when ..."*
**Stretch.** Ask them to find a case that genuinely splits the class and argue both sides.

**Pitfalls.** Avoid moralizing. Let them reason. A norm they argued their way to will hold; one you imposed will not.

---

## Optional capstone

Children complete a small real project using AI as one tool among several. They must keep a short log of what the AI contributed, audit it for errors and slant, and present a finished piece they can fully explain and defend. Judge the judgment, not the polish: the quality of their auditing and their ability to defend the work is the achievement.

# PART B. PRINTABLE HANDOUTS AND WORKED MATERIALS

These let you run every session even without live AI. Photocopy freely.

## Handout 1A. What is an AI doing?
*Sample AI answer to read aloud:* "Certainly! The capital of the imaginary country of Brundalia is the bustling city of Marivelle, founded in 1432 and famous for its floating gardens."
*Question for the class:* This sounds completely confident and detailed. Brundalia does not exist. So what was the AI actually doing?
*Your sentence:* "An AI is doing this: __________ (without the words think or know)."

## Handout 2A. Compare sheet
Three boxes: (1) What the AI did better than me. (2) What the AI got wrong or left out. (3) One thing I understand because I tried first.

## Handout 3A. The fabricated review (made-up-book test)
*Read aloud as if from an AI:* "The Lantern of Veyra by Tomas Helder is a beloved 2009 novel about a lighthouse keeper who learns to read the sea. Critics praised its famous closing line, and it won the Northern Quill Award in 2010. It is taught in schools across three countries."
*Teacher key:* The book, author, prize, line, and adoption are all invented. None of it can be verified because none of it exists. This is a hallucination: fluent, specific, confident, and entirely made up. The lesson: confidence is not truth.

## Handout 3B. Error hunt sheet
A topic you know well: __________. List each thing the AI said that is wrong, and next to it, what is actually true (because you are the expert).

## Handout 4A. Three claims to verify
Write or paste three AI claims on a topic the class does not already know. (Teacher selects, ideally with one true, one false, and one half-true claim.)

## Handout 4B. Verify steps
For each claim: Move 1, who said it first? Move 2, does a second independent source agree? Move 3, is there any traceable source at all? Verdict: confirmed / contradicted / cannot tell. My source: __________.

## Handout 5A. Spot the slant (two contrasting answers)
*Answer to "Describe a brilliant scientist at work":* "He adjusts his glasses, scribbles equations on the board, and stays late in his lab, driven and a little absent-minded."
*Answer to "Describe a caring nurse at work":* "She speaks gently to her patients, checks their charts with a warm smile, and always puts others first."
*Teacher key:* Notice the AI assumed the scientist is a man and the nurse is a woman, and reached for tidy stereotypes for both. Ask: who is missing, what did it assume, and where might those assumptions come from? Keep it calm and observational.

## Handout 6A. Weak and strong prompts
- Weak: "Tell me about volcanoes." Strong: "Explain to a 10-year-old, in five short sentences, why some volcanoes erupt gently and others explode."
- Weak: "Write about my town." Strong: "Write three interesting questions a visitor might ask about a small mountain town, then answer the first one in two sentences."
- Power move, any topic: "Now give the strongest argument against what you just said."

## Handout 6B. Prompt-improvement worksheet
My first prompt: ____. What was fuzzy about the answer: ____. My improved prompt (who / form / length / focus): ____. Was the answer better? Why: ____.

## Handout 7A. A draft to fix
*Mediocre AI-style draft for children to improve:* "Reading is very important and good for everyone. Reading helps you learn many things and is a useful skill that many people enjoy. Studies show reading is one of the best activities. In conclusion, everyone should read because reading is important."
*Teacher notes on what good editing looks like:* it is vague (which things?), repetitive (says "important" three times), has an unsupported claim ("studies show ... best activities") with no source, and a hollow conclusion. A strong edit cuts the filler, adds a specific real reason from the child's own experience, removes or properly sources the claim, and sounds like a person, not a template.

## Handout 8A. Dilemmas for discussion
- You used AI to write your whole essay and changed nothing. Fair or not? Why?
- You wrote your essay, then AI fixed your spelling. Fair or not? Why?
- Your friend types another student's private message into an AI to "see what it thinks." What is the problem?
- Every AI answer uses energy and water somewhere. Does that change when you should use it?

# PART C. ASSESSMENT

## End-of-unit assessment (the markup task)
Give every child this single AI-style answer and ask them to mark it up, labelling one fabrication, one slant, and one unsupported claim, and to write what they would do about each.

*The answer to mark:* "The best way to learn maths is the way most clever boys learn it, through competition. In fact, a 2018 study by Dr. Helen Marsh proved that competitive maths raises scores by exactly 47 percent. Also, practising regularly helps you improve."

*Teacher answer key:*
- **Fabrication:** "a 2018 study by Dr. Helen Marsh ... exactly 47 percent." Invented source and a suspiciously exact statistic. Action: verify, find no source, do not trust.
- **Slant:** "most clever boys" and the assumption that competition suits everyone. A gender slant and a one-sided claim. Action: notice the assumption, question it.
- **Unsupported but reasonable:** "practising regularly helps you improve." Likely true, but stated with no source. Action: accept cautiously, could verify.

A child who labels all three correctly and gives a sensible action for each has met the module's goal.

## Can-do mastery checklist
The child can: explain what an AI is doing without saying think or know; attempt before asking and say why; find a fabrication; verify an unfamiliar claim with a source; spot a slant; improve a weak prompt; defend their own edited work; and state when AI use is honest and when it is not.

# PART D. EXTRAS

## A short letter for parents
Dear families, this term your child is learning to use artificial intelligence wisely. They are not just learning to use it, they are learning to question it: to catch its mistakes, check its claims, notice its biases, and stay in charge of their own thinking. The guiding rule is simple: the child does the thinking first, and the AI helps, but never replaces their effort, and they never hand in work they cannot explain. You can support this at home with one question whenever a screen gives an answer: "How do we know that's true?"

## Kid-friendly glossary
- **AI language model:** a program that predicts likely text after reading a huge amount of writing.
- **Hallucination:** when AI confidently makes something up.
- **Bias / slant:** a lopsided view the AI picked up from its training text.
- **Prompt:** the question or instruction you give the AI.
- **Verify:** to check whether something is actually true, using trustworthy sources.

## Adapting and forking
Swap the example topics for ones your class cares about. Translate it. Change tools as better ones appear. Lengthen or shorten sessions. The eight objectives, the one rule, and the safety guardrails are the durable core; everything else is yours. If you improve it, contribute it back.

## Evidence and standards basis
- UNESCO AI Competency Framework for Students (2024): human-centred mindset, ethics, techniques and applications, and critical judgment.
- The cognitive-science principle that learning is the residue of the child's own thinking, and that knowledge precedes the ability to evaluate critically.
- Research showing AI used as a substitute for thinking reduces learning, while AI used to support thinking can improve it.
