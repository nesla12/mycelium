# The Building Years: Reading and Mathematics

### How to teach the core of childhood, ages 6 to 10, explicitly and well
#### Part of the Human-Core Initiative

These are the years that decide most of what follows. A child who leaves them reading fluently and with a secure sense of number can learn almost anything next. A child who does not is in trouble that compounds. The good news: we know how to teach both, and it is not mysterious. It is explicit, it is sequenced, and it works best for exactly the children who are usually left behind.

This is the do-Monday version. It tells you the order to teach things in, what good looks like with real numbers, and the shape of a lesson you can run tomorrow.

---

## The shape of a good lesson

Almost any new skill or knowledge in these years is taught well in the same shape, drawn from Barak Rosenshine's *Principles of Instruction* (2012), a plain summary of what the most effective teachers actually do. You do not need all ten principles memorised. You need this loop:

1. **Start with retrieval.** Open every lesson with a few quick questions on earlier material. Not new, not graded, just "get it out of your memory." This is the highest-value five minutes in the day.
2. **Teach in small steps.** Present a little, then let them practise it, before adding the next piece. Do not talk for twenty minutes and then set a task.
3. **Model it. Show worked examples.** Think aloud as you do the thing. Show finished and half-finished examples. Novices learn far more from studying a worked example than from struggling alone.
4. **Ask lots of questions, and check everyone.** Not "does anyone have a question?" but "everyone, show me on your whiteboard." You are checking the whole room understood before moving on, aiming for a high success rate of around eighty percent.
5. **Guide their practice, then release it.** Practise together with feedback, then let them do it independently once they are succeeding.
6. **Come back to it.** Review across the week and the month. Things half-learned and never revisited are things forgotten.

That loop is the whole method. The rest of this guide is what to pour into it.

# Reading

A child reads when two things are both in place: they can turn print into words (decoding), and they have the language and knowledge to understand those words (comprehension). This is the **Simple View of Reading**: comprehension is decoding multiplied by language comprehension, and if either is near zero, reading fails. You build them differently, so we take them in turn.

## 1. Decoding: systematic synthetic phonics

Teach the relationships between letters and sounds directly and in a planned order, until reading is fluent and automatic. Do not teach children to guess words from the picture or the first letter; that is the habit of struggling readers, not skilled ones.

**Where to start, concretely.** Do not teach the alphabet in order. Start with a small set of high-utility letter-sounds and build fast. The classic, well-tested first set is:

> **s, a, t, p, i, n**

because with just those six a child can already read and spell real words: *sat, tap, pin, nap, tip, pan, sit, nip*. From the very first week, reading is decoding real words, not reciting letter names.

**The order after that.** Single letters and their sounds first, then the trickier combinations. A common, sensible sequence:

1. The first sets of single consonants and short vowels (s a t p i n, then m d g o c k, then e u r h b f l, and the rest).
2. Blending those into CVC words (consonant-vowel-consonant: *cat, dog, run*).
3. Consonant digraphs, two letters making one sound (*ck, sh, ch, th, ng*).
4. Words with consonant clusters (*stop, frog, jump, best*).
5. Long vowels with the silent e (*cake, bike, hope*).
6. Vowel digraphs (*rain, may, boat, see*).
7. R-controlled vowels (*car, her, bird*).
8. The rest of the complex code (*oi/oy, oo, igh*, and the many spellings of each sound).

Introduce roughly three to six new letter-sounds a week, faster or slower depending on the children, and as the saying goes, go "as fast as you can but as slow as you must." Teach two moves in lockstep: **blending** (run the sounds together to read a word) and **segmenting** (break a word into sounds to spell it).

**Use decodable books.** The first books a child reads should contain only the letter-sounds they have been taught, so they practise decoding and succeed, rather than guessing. As soon as decoding is secure, move them to real, rich books of every kind.

## 2. Fluency: accurate, automatic, expressive

Decoding has to become automatic, so that no mental effort is spent on the words and all of it is free for meaning. Fluency has three parts: accuracy, a good pace, and reading with expression.

**How to build it.** The single best method is rereading. The child reads a short passage at the right level, then reads it again, two or three times, getting smoother each time. Model it first by reading aloud yourself. Read together (you and the child in unison), or take turns. Never make a child cold-read aloud to the class from text they have not seen; it humiliates and teaches nothing.

**What good looks like, in numbers.** Oral reading fluency is usually measured in words read correctly per minute (WCPM) from an unpractised passage. As a rough guide, the widely used Hasbrouck and Tindal norms put a typical child at the end of the year at roughly:

- about 60 WCPM after the first year of school,
- about 100 after the second,
- about 110 to 115 after the third,
- about 130 after the fourth.

These are averages on English-language passages, useful as a check, not a target to drill. The practical rule from the same research: a child reading more than about ten words per minute below the typical level for their stage needs a deliberate fluency-building program, not just more time.

## 3. Comprehension: built from knowledge, not drilled

Here is the part most schools get wrong. Once a child can decode fluently, how much they understand is driven almost entirely by **vocabulary and background knowledge**, not by practising "comprehension skills" in the abstract. A child understands a passage about a topic they know far better than a stronger reader who knows nothing about it.

So the way to build comprehension is to **build knowledge**. Read widely and deliberately across science, history, geography, art, and stories. Teach a rich, connected body of knowledge on purpose. Talk about what you read. Pre-teach the few hard words. The "skill" of comprehension largely takes care of itself once the knowledge is there.

## The daily reading routine

- Five to ten minutes of phonics, taught in the lesson shape above, with new sounds and review of old ones.
- Decoding practice in decodable text (early on), then rereading for fluency.
- Daily reading aloud to the children from rich books above their own reading level, to feed vocabulary and knowledge.
- Talk. Always talk about what was read.

# Mathematics

The goal of these years is a child who has a secure sense of number, can recall the basic facts without thinking, and understands what they are doing rather than copying a procedure. Two ideas carry most of the weight.

## 1. Concrete, then pictorial, then abstract

Introduce every new idea in three stages, the backbone of Singapore's mastery mathematics:

- **Concrete:** the child handles real objects. Counters, blocks, coins, their own fingers.
- **Pictorial:** the idea is drawn. Dots, a number line, a diagram.
- **Abstract:** finally the symbols, *7 + 5 = 12*.

Do not rush to the symbols. A child who has only ever seen the abstract is copying, not understanding. And teach for **mastery**: do not move on until the current step is secure, because maths is a tower and a missing brick brings down everything above it.

## 2. Fluency: the facts that must become automatic

Reasoning needs working memory, and working memory is tiny. Every fact a child has to stop and work out is memory not available for the actual problem. So a small set of facts must become **automatic**, recalled instantly and effortlessly, in this order:

1. **Number bonds first.** The pairs that make ten (*6 + 4, 7 + 3*), then the pairs within and to twenty. These are the foundation of all mental arithmetic.
2. **Then the times tables**, in a sensible order rather than 1 to 12 in turn. A common, effective sequence: **2, 5, and 10 first** (their patterns are easy and motivating), then **3, 4, and 8**, then **6, 7, and 9**, and finally **11 and 12** (which can be derived from the ten times table). Division facts come alongside, as the same fact read backwards.

Automaticity is built by short, daily, low-stakes retrieval, not by one long test. A few minutes a day, every day, beats an hour once a week.

## 3. The bar model: making word problems visible

The bar model is the single most useful technique to teach, because it turns a confusing word problem into a picture a child can reason about. You draw the quantities as bars.

> *Sara has 12 sweets. She gives 5 to her brother. How many are left?*
> Draw one bar of 12. Mark off a part of 5. The unknown part is what is left. The child sees that this is *12 minus 5*, and why.

Word problems stop being a guessing game about which operation to use, because the picture shows the relationship before any calculating begins.

## 4. Practice that actually sticks

Three findings from cognitive science, in plain practice:

- **Retrieval.** Start each lesson by recalling facts from memory, books closed. The act of retrieving strengthens the memory far more than being shown the fact again.
- **Spacing.** Return to old topics across the weeks, rather than teaching something once and leaving it forever.
- **Interleaving.** Mix problem types in practice instead of doing twenty of the same kind in a block. It feels harder, and it builds far stronger problem-solving, because the child has to decide *which* method a problem needs, not just run the one they were just shown (Rohrer and colleagues). A simple daily starter of five mixed questions does all three at once.

## The daily maths routine

- A five-question mixed retrieval starter (number bonds, a couple of tables, one from last week, one from last term).
- New teaching in the lesson shape, concrete to pictorial to abstract, with worked examples.
- Guided then independent practice, with the bar model for any word problems.
- Keep success high. If most of the room is failing, the step was too big; go back.

# Writing, briefly

Reading's quieter twin. Teach **handwriting explicitly**; forming letters by hand helps fix the letters themselves and frees attention for composing later. Build writing from the **sentence up**, with clear models, before paragraphs and essays. And have children **write about what they are learning** across every subject, because writing is also how you find out whether they understood it.

## How to tell it is working

You do not need heavy testing. You need cheap, frequent signals.

- **Listen to them read aloud.** Halting, guessing, or sounding out every word means more phonics and more fluency practice, not more pressure. Smooth and expressive means move on to richer text.
- **Check the facts are automatic.** Ask a number bond or a table fact and count the seconds. If they have to work it out, it is not yet automatic, so keep practising it.
- **Can they explain it?** A child who can teach a method to someone else understands it. One who can only copy the steps does not.
- **Look at a folder of their work over months,** not a single score on a single day.
- Use the fluency benchmarks above as a rough check on who needs help early, while there is still time to give it.

## What to avoid

The evidence is as clear about what does not work as what does.

- **Guessing from pictures or context** instead of decoding. It is the habit of poor readers; do not teach it.
- **"Comprehension skills" drilled in the abstract,** detached from knowledge. Build knowledge instead.
- **Matching teaching to "learning styles."** It has been tested repeatedly and does not work.
- **Expecting children to discover methods for themselves.** Novices need to be shown; discovery learning is for experts with knowledge to build on.
- **Rereading and highlighting as the main study method.** Among the least effective things a learner can do. Replace with retrieval.
- **Rushing to the abstract in maths,** or moving on before a step is secure.
